Data in Peripleo comes from many sources. Our sources often publish information about the same real-world thing. A major historical place like Rome for example, does not just appear in one or two of our partners' datasets - it appears in many.

Linked Data

Luckily, our partners publish their data as Linked Data. Linked Data is a way of connecting online resources that have some element in common. It uses typed links (i.e. links with a meaning attached to them) to declare how different pieces of data relate to one another; or when they are about the same real-world thing.

Peripleo uses these links to connect equivalent records. Therefore, when you select a place in Peripleo, the information that you see - image, names, description, dates - is usually not from one specific authority. Instead, it is an aggregate of information, published collectively by members of the Pelagios Commons community.

Peripleo's info boxes, by design, provide only a compact overview. Ready to dive deeper into the actual Linked Data that underpins them? Let's take a closer look.

Looking Underneath the Hood

Let's start by selecting the place Lepcis Magna. The row of coloured codes you see at the bottom are a hint to the Linked Data nature of our information: each code is an identifier for Lepcis Magna in one of our partner datasets. What looks like a single entry for Lepcis Magna in Peripleo, is actually the combination of these individual partner records.

Click the  icon next to the coloured identifiers. This opens the Linked Data View. It consists of the link graph (left) and the record details (right).

The Link Graph

The link graph shows how our individual records connect to each other. The record in the Digital Atlas of the Roman Empire dare:21127 links to its counterpart in the Pleiades Gazetteer of the Ancient World pleiades:344448; Archaeological Atlas of Antiquity Vici.org vici:2448 links to to Pleiades as well as to the equivalent Wikidata record, and so on.

Exact vs. Close Match

In terms of indicating equivalence, Pelagios partners use two different types of links. (The types are defined through a Linked Data vocabulary called the Simple Knowledge Organization System SKOS.) One of the types indicates an exact match (solid line), meaning the record is conceptually identical - it represents the same place in terms of geographical, cultural and temporal scope. The other link indicates a close match (dashed line), which means the records are pretty close, but not necessarily the exact same thing. Our partners use this type, among other things, to connect an ancient place in their dataset to its modern successor in another.

Indexed vs. External Records

Coloured dots in the graph represent records that Peripleo has indexed. White dots, on the other hand, are links to external datasets. Partners link to them in their records, but Peripleo does currently not harvest any further information from them.

Record Details

For records that include coordinates, the map shows colour coded markers. (Use the  button to switch base layers.) Underneath the map you can find the details for earch individual record, as they were originally provided by the partner.

You will notice that locations, as well as other data will differ, depending which dataset they come from. The power of Linked Data lies in the fact that variety - and even contradiction - is perfectly fine & possible. Peripleo's main interface needs to conceal much of this diversity for the sake of keeping interaction simple. Therefore, always be sure to dig down into the Linked Data view, as well as visit the original sources when exploring Pelagios Commons.
